What it was like to study Quantum Turbulence for a PhD

A journey through my doctoral research on quantum turbulence in superfluid helium at absolute zero temperature, combining computational modeling with experimental techniques.

Matthew J. Doyle Quantum Turbulence Researcher

Contents

Introduction

In 2024, I completed my doctoral thesis at the University of Manchester, focusing on quantum turbulence in superfluid helium-4 in the zero temperature limit. This research combined advanced numerical simulations with experimental techniques to understand the behavior of quantum vortices in superfluid flows.

Adapting to Challenges

The COVID-19 pandemic significantly impacted the initial direction of my research. During the first 4-5 months, laboratory access was restricted, leading to a strategic pivot in my research approach. While my original plan involved a pipe flow experiment to study superfluid helium-4 pressure drop at absolute zero temperature, circumstances led to a greater focus on numerical simulations.

The challenges extended beyond just access restrictions - the reduced availability and increasing costs of liquid helium meant that running multiple simultaneous experiments became unfeasible. This situation prompted an evolution in my research methodology.

Computational Investigations

The bulk of my thesis focused on numerical simulations of quantum vortices in superfluid helium-4 at absolute zero temperature. These simulations provided crucial insights into vortex-surface interactions and the dynamics of quantum turbulence. The computational approach allowed us to explore phenomena that would be challenging to observe directly in experimental settings.

Experimental Work

Following the cancellation of the original pipe flow experiment, I contributed to an alternative experimental project within our research group. This work focused on visualizing quantum vortices in superfluid helium-4 at absolute zero temperature. Interestingly, the scale of tracer particles examined in these experiments was comparable to the surface roughness considered in my simulation work, creating an unexpected bridge between the computational and experimental aspects of my research.

Research Impact

Despite the unexpected challenges and changes in direction, both the computational and experimental studies contributed significantly to our understanding of quantum turbulence in superfluid helium-4 at absolute zero temperature. The research particularly advanced our knowledge of vortex-surface pinning and vortex-particle binding mechanics, demonstrating how adaptability in research methodology can lead to valuable scientific insights.
